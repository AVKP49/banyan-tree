# The Elephant and the Blind Men
## A Jain and Buddhist Folk Tale — Adapted for The Banyan Tree

[voice: dadi]
[music: meditative sitar and soft bells, morning ambience]

## Opening Framing

[sfx: morning market sounds, distant temple bell]

Come, beta, sit with Dadi. I want to ask you something before we start tonight's story. If I asked you to describe an elephant, what would you say? Big? Gray? Has a trunk?

[pause]

But what if you could only touch one part of the elephant — only one — and you had to guess what the whole animal was like? What would you say then?

[pause]

Tonight I want to tell you a very famous story from India. It is an ancient tale — so ancient that Jain teachers, Buddhist monks, Hindu scholars, and Sufi poets have all told their own version. Everyone loves this story, beta, because it teaches us something we all need to learn. Even grown-ups. Especially grown-ups.

[pause]

This is the story of an elephant and six blind men. And it's about how we see the world — or think we see the world. Let's begin.

[music: transition to storytelling theme]

## The Story

[sfx: bustling town, voices, animals]

[voice: dadi]
Once upon a time, in a busy little town in India, there lived six men. They were all blind — they had never seen the world with their eyes. But beta, these were not helpless men. No, no. They were scholars. They were proud of how much they knew. They argued about philosophy and science and the nature of the world, and each one believed he was the wisest of all.

[pause]

One day, they heard that the rajah — the king of their town — had a new elephant. A magnificent elephant, brought all the way from the deep forests.

[voice: first-man]
"An elephant!" said the first man. "I have read about elephants in many books. But I have never touched one. Let us go and discover what an elephant truly is."

[voice: dadi]
And so the six blind men walked together to the rajah's courtyard, where the great elephant stood, calm and patient, munching on sugarcane.

[sfx: elephant breathing, chain clinking gently, sugarcane crunching]

[voice: dadi]
The rajah's keeper led them forward. "Go ahead," he said. "Touch the elephant. Learn what he is."

The first man walked forward and reached out his hands. His palms pressed against something wide and flat and rough — like a warm, breathing wall.

[voice: first-man]
"Ah ha!" he cried. "An elephant is like a wall! Broad, strong, and solid!"

[voice: dadi]
The second man reached out and found something smooth, round, and hard — the elephant's tusk.

[voice: second-man]
"No, no, you are completely wrong! An elephant is like a spear — smooth and sharp and pointed!"

[sfx: men murmuring]

[voice: dadi]
The third man came forward and found the elephant's trunk. It was long, muscular, and it moved — it wrapped around his hand gently.

[voice: third-man]
"You are both fools! An elephant is like a great snake! It is long and round and it moves on its own!"

[music: building curiosity theme]

[voice: dadi]
The fourth man walked to the elephant's leg and wrapped his arms around it. It was thick, rough, and solid as a tree.

[voice: fourth-man]
"I am certain now — an elephant is like a tree trunk! Thick and round and strong, planted firmly in the earth!"

[voice: dadi]
The fifth man reached up high and felt the elephant's ear — wide, thin, and flapping gently in the breeze.

[voice: fifth-man]
"How can you all be so wrong? An elephant is like a fan! Thin and flat and it moves the air!"

[voice: dadi]
And the sixth man? He grabbed the elephant's tail — thin, rough, with a tuft of hair at the end.

[voice: sixth-man]
"I have found the truth at last! An elephant is like a rope! You are all mistaken!"

[sfx: rising voices, argument]

[voice: dadi]
And then, beta — they started arguing. Oh, what an argument it was!

"A wall!" "A spear!" "A snake!" "A tree!" "A fan!" "A rope!"

[pause]

They shouted. They waved their hands. Each man was absolutely sure — completely, totally, one hundred percent sure — that he was right and everyone else was wrong. The argument got louder and louder, until the whole courtyard was filled with their shouting.

[sfx: loud arguing, then a staff tapping three times on stone]

[voice: dadi]
And then — tap, tap, tap — a calm voice spoke.

[music: wise, serene theme]

[voice: wise-woman]
"Peace, friends. Peace."

[voice: dadi]
It was an old woman — a wise woman who sat in the shade of a neem tree near the courtyard. She had been listening to every word.

[voice: wise-woman]
"You are all right. And you are all wrong."

[pause]

[voice: dadi]
The six men fell silent. "What do you mean?" they asked.

[voice: wise-woman]
"Each of you touched one part of the elephant. The elephant is all of these things — and more. He is the wall AND the spear AND the snake AND the tree AND the fan AND the rope. He is all of these, together, at once. You each discovered one truth, but you mistook your small truth for the whole truth. And that, my friends, is not wisdom — that is blindness of a different kind."

[pause]

[voice: dadi]
Beta, the six men stood very still. And then, slowly, something changed. They stopped shouting. They started listening. Each man described what he had felt, and together — only together — they began to understand the whole elephant.

[sfx: gentle murmuring, agreement, soft laughter]

[voice: dadi]
The first man's wall was the elephant's side. The second man's spear was the tusk. The third man's snake was the trunk. The tree was the leg, the fan was the ear, and the rope was the tail. Each man had been right about his part. But no one had been right about the whole.

[music: warm, harmonious resolution]

[voice: dadi]
And from that day on, beta, those six men argued a little less and listened a little more.

## Wisdom Moment

[music: gentle reflection]

[voice: dadi]
Beta, this is one of the most important stories Dadi knows. And I'll tell you why.

[pause]

Every person in this world sees things from where they are standing. Your friend might see something one way. You might see it another way. And both of you might be right — but only about a part. The whole truth is bigger than what any one person can see.

[pause]

So when someone disagrees with you — before you argue, before you shout, before you say "you're wrong" — stop and think: maybe they are touching a different part of the elephant. Maybe if you listen to them, and they listen to you, together you can see the whole.

That's wisdom, beta. Real wisdom.

## Bilingual Coda

[music: soft, warm]

[voice: dadi]
Now, beta, Dadi wants to teach you a word. The word is "haathi." Say it with me — haa-thi.

[pause]

Haathi.

[pause]

"Haathi" means elephant in Hindi. In India, haathis are very special. They are part of festivals — decorated with bright paints and flowers and golden cloths, they walk in parades while people celebrate. Lord Ganesha, one of the most beloved gods in Hinduism, has the head of a haathi. He is the god of wisdom and new beginnings.

So when you say "haathi," beta, you are saying a word that is connected to celebrations, to wisdom, and to something very, very big — just like the truth.

## Closing

[voice: dadi]
Thank you for sitting with me tonight, beta. This story is very close to my heart. My dadi told it to me when I was arguing with my sister — I was so sure I was right! And my dadi said, "Beta, maybe you are both touching a different part of the haathi."

[pause]

I never forgot that. And now you won't forget it either.

Good night, beta. Sleep well. And if you have a question for Dadi — anything at all — just ask me. I'm right here, under the banyan tree.

[sfx: temple bells in distance, evening birds, fade to silence]
