# The Monkey and the Crocodile
## A Panchatantra Tale — Adapted for The Banyan Tree

[voice: dadi]
[music: gentle sitar and tanpura, warm evening ambience]

## Opening Framing

[sfx: evening birds, gentle river flowing]

Come, beta, sit with me. Do you see that big tree over there? In my village, we had a tree just like that — a great banyan tree, so old that even my dadi's dadi didn't know when it was planted. Its roots hung down like curtains, and its branches spread wider than our whole house.

[pause]

Tonight I want to tell you a story about a tree like that — a jambul tree, full of the sweetest purple fruit you can imagine — and about two friends who met beside a river. One was a monkey, and one was a crocodile. And what happened between them — well, beta, it will make you think about what it means to be a true friend.

[pause]

This story comes from the Panchatantra — that's a very old book of wise stories from India, older than a thousand years. My dadi told me this story under our banyan tree, and now I'm telling it to you.

[music: transition to storytelling theme]

## The Story

[sfx: river flowing, jungle ambience]

[voice: dadi]
Once upon a time, by the banks of a wide, shimmering river, there stood a magnificent jambul tree. Its branches were heavy with ripe purple fruit — so sweet, so juicy, that the smell would drift all the way across the water.

[pause]

And in that tree lived a monkey. He was a clever fellow — quick and bright, with kind eyes and a generous heart. Every morning he would swing from branch to branch, picking the ripest jambul fruit and eating until his cheeks were purple.

[sfx: monkey chattering softly, branches rustling]

[voice: dadi]
One day, a crocodile came swimming up the river. He was tired and hungry. He crawled onto the bank beneath the jambul tree and rested there, his long snout pointing up at the sky.

[voice: monkey]
"Hello there, friend! You look hungry. Would you like some jambul fruit? They're the sweetest fruit in the whole jungle!"

[sfx: fruit dropping, splashing in water]

[voice: dadi]
The monkey tossed down a handful of ripe jambul, and the crocodile ate them gratefully. Oh beta, they were delicious — sweet as honey, cool as monsoon rain.

[pause]

And so, day after day, the monkey and the crocodile met by the river. The monkey would share his fruit, and the crocodile would tell stories about the deep parts of the river where the fish swam silver. They became friends — real friends, the kind who are happy just sitting together.

[music: warm friendship theme]

[voice: dadi]
One evening, the crocodile brought some jambul fruit home to his wife.

[voice: crocodile]
"Taste this, dear — my friend the monkey gives me these every day. Aren't they wonderful?"

[voice: dadi]
The crocodile's wife ate the fruit, and her eyes went wide.

[sfx: subtle tension music begins]

"These are indeed very sweet," she said, thinking quietly. "But tell me — if the monkey eats such sweet fruit every single day, imagine how sweet his heart must be."

[pause]

[voice: dadi]
Beta, can you believe what she said? She wanted the monkey's heart. She told her husband: "Bring me the monkey's heart, or don't come home at all."

The poor crocodile was torn. The monkey was his friend — his dear, generous friend who had shared fruit with him every single day. But his wife was angry, and she would not listen to reason.

[pause]

[music: tension rising]

The next morning, the crocodile swam to the jambul tree with a heavy heart.

[voice: crocodile]
"Friend, my wife loved the fruit you sent! She wants to invite you to our home for a meal. Will you come? I'll carry you across the river on my back."

[voice: monkey]
"Really? How wonderful! I've never been across the river before!"

[voice: dadi]
The monkey hopped onto the crocodile's back, and off they went, gliding across the wide water. The river sparkled in the morning sun, and the monkey chattered happily.

[sfx: water flowing, gentle splashing]

But halfway across the river, the crocodile began to sink — slowly, deliberately — deeper and deeper into the water.

[voice: monkey]
"Friend! What are you doing? I can't swim! Why are you going deeper?"

[pause]

[voice: crocodile]
"I'm sorry, friend. My wife wants your heart. She says it must be as sweet as the fruit you eat. I have to bring it to her."

[sfx: dramatic pause in music]

[voice: dadi]
Now, beta — what do you think the monkey felt? Scared? Betrayed? Of course he did. But the monkey was clever — remember, I told you he was clever. And sometimes, beta, being clever can save your life.

[pause]

The monkey took a deep breath. And then — instead of panicking — he laughed.

[voice: monkey]
"Oh, my friend! Why didn't you tell me earlier? I would have brought my heart with me! You see, we monkeys don't carry our hearts inside us. We keep them safe in the hollow of our tree. Quick — take me back to the jambul tree, and I'll fetch it for you!"

[voice: dadi]
Now, beta, the crocodile wasn't as clever as the monkey. He believed every word. He turned around and swam as fast as he could back to the jambul tree.

[sfx: fast water movement]

The moment they reached the bank, the monkey leaped — whoosh! — up into the highest branch of the jambul tree, safe among the leaves.

[sfx: branches rustling, monkey landing]

[voice: dadi]
The crocodile waited below, looking up.

[voice: crocodile]
"Well? Where is your heart? Bring it down!"

[pause]

[voice: monkey]
"Oh, my friend. No creature in this world keeps its heart outside its body. My heart is right here, inside me, where it has always been. And a true friend — a real friend — would never have asked for it."

[music: gentle, bittersweet theme]

[voice: dadi]
The monkey looked down at the crocodile with sad eyes — not angry eyes, beta, but sad ones. Because he had lost a friend. Not because the crocodile was wicked, but because the crocodile had been weak.

[pause]

The crocodile hung his head and swam away. And the monkey sat in his jambul tree and ate his sweet fruit alone.

[sfx: evening birds, river flowing gently]

## Wisdom Moment

[music: gentle reflection]

[voice: dadi]
Beta, what do you think? What is a true friend?

[pause]

A true friend shares with you. A true friend is honest with you. And a true friend — a real one — would never ask you for something that would hurt you. The monkey knew that. And now you know it too.

[pause]

## Bilingual Coda

[music: soft, warm]

[voice: dadi]
Now, beta, Dadi wants to teach you a word. The word is "jungle." Say it with me — jun-gle.

[pause]

Jun-gle.

[pause]

"Jungle" is actually a Hindi word — it comes from the Sanskrit word "jangala." It means a thick forest where animals live freely — just like the forest where our monkey lived in his jambul tree. In India, there are many beautiful jungles, full of tigers, elephants, peacocks, and monkeys just like our clever friend.

Next time you hear the word "jungle," you'll know — it's an Indian word. Isn't that wonderful?

## Closing

[voice: dadi]
Thank you for sitting with me tonight, beta. Tomorrow, I have another story for you — this one is about a very clever man named Birbal and a pot of khichdi. You'll love it.

[pause]

Good night, beta. Sleep well. And if you have a question for Dadi — anything at all — just ask me. I'm right here, under the banyan tree.
