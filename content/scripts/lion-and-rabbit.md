# The Lion and the Rabbit
## A Panchatantra Tale — Adapted for The Banyan Tree

[voice: dadi]
[music: deep forest drums, tanpura drone]

## Opening Framing

[sfx: jungle ambience, distant roar]

Come, beta, come sit close to Dadi tonight. I have a story for you — a very old story, from the Panchatantra. You remember I told you about the Panchatantra? Those wise, wise stories from India, older than a thousand years?

[pause]

Tonight's story is about a lion — a big, proud, terrible lion — and a tiny rabbit. Now, beta, who do you think wins — the strongest animal in the jungle, or the smallest? Hmm? Let me tell you.

[pause]

In my village, my dadi used to say: "The strongest arm is not always the one that wins. Sometimes it is the sharpest mind." And this story — oh, this story proves it. Are you ready? Then let's begin.

[music: transition to storytelling theme]

## The Story

[sfx: heavy footsteps, jungle tension]

[voice: dadi]
Once upon a time, deep in a thick, green jungle, there lived a lion. He was enormous — golden fur, great yellow eyes, and a roar so loud it shook the leaves from the trees. His name was Bhasuraka, and he was the king of that jungle.

[pause]

But beta, Bhasuraka was not a good king. He was cruel. Every day he would hunt — not because he was hungry, but because he could. He killed deer and rabbits and birds and fish, far more than he could ever eat. The animals lived in constant fear.

[sfx: frightened animal sounds, birds scattering]

[voice: dadi]
One day, the animals gathered together — the deer, the rabbits, the elephants, the birds, everyone. They were tired of being afraid.

[voice: deer]
"We cannot go on like this. Every day he kills so many of us. If he only took one animal a day, at least the rest of us could live in peace."

[voice: dadi]
So the animals went to the lion with a proposal.

[voice: deer]
"O mighty Bhasuraka, you are our king. We will send one animal to you every day, so you do not need to hunt. You can rest in your cave, and your meal will come to you."

[sfx: low growl]

[voice: lion]
"Hmm. One animal, delivered to me every day? I won't have to lift a paw? Fine. But if even one day you fail to send someone — I will destroy every last one of you."

[music: dark, ominous]

[voice: dadi]
And so, beta, every day, the animals drew lots, and one poor creature would walk slowly, sadly, to the lion's cave. It was terrible. But at least the others were safe — for that day.

[pause]

Weeks passed. Months passed. And then one morning, the lot fell to a small rabbit. He was young, clever, and very, very calm.

[sfx: gentle breeze, birds singing]

[voice: dadi]
The other animals wept for him. "Poor little rabbit," they said. "So small. So young."

But the rabbit? Beta, the rabbit was thinking. He sat quietly, his nose twitching, his eyes bright. And then he smiled.

[voice: rabbit]
"Don't cry for me. I have a plan. But I need you to trust me — and I need to be a little bit late."

[pause]

[voice: dadi]
Instead of going straight to the lion, the rabbit took his time. He hopped slowly through the jungle, nibbled some grass, sat by a stream. He waited until the sun was high and hot, and the lion's belly was growling with hunger.

[sfx: angry roar in distance]

[voice: dadi]
When the rabbit finally arrived at the lion's cave, Bhasuraka was furious. His mane was bristling. His eyes were burning.

[voice: lion]
"You! You tiny thing! You dare to come late? I will eat you in one bite — and then I will go and destroy every animal in this jungle!"

[voice: dadi]
But the rabbit bowed very low and spoke in a trembling voice — though, beta, between you and me, I think the trembling was just an act.

[voice: rabbit]
"O great king, forgive me! I did not come late because I was careless. There were six of us rabbits sent to you today — a grand feast! But on the way here, we met another lion. A terrible lion! Even bigger than you!"

[sfx: tension music rising]

[voice: lion]
"Another lion? In MY jungle?"

[voice: rabbit]
"Yes, Your Majesty! He was huge and fierce. He ate five of my companions right in front of me and said — oh, I am afraid to say it—"

[voice: lion]
"Tell me! What did he say?"

[voice: rabbit]
"He said, 'There is no real king in this jungle. That Bhasuraka is nothing but a weak old cat.' I barely escaped to warn you!"

[music: dramatic crescendo]

[voice: dadi]
Oh, beta, the lion was shaking with rage. No one calls a lion a cat! No one!

[voice: lion]
"Take me to this pretender! I will tear him apart!"

[voice: rabbit]
"Follow me, Your Majesty."

[voice: dadi]
The clever little rabbit led the lion through the jungle — past the tall trees, past the river, until they came to an old, deep well. The water inside was dark and still as a mirror.

[sfx: footsteps stopping, eerie silence]

[voice: rabbit]
"He lives in there, Your Majesty. In that fortress. Look inside — you will see him."

[voice: dadi]
The lion crept to the edge of the well and peered down. And there, staring back at him from the dark water — was a lion. A big, fierce, angry-looking lion.

[pause]

It was his own reflection, beta. But the lion didn't know that.

[voice: lion]
"THERE YOU ARE!"

[sfx: tremendous roar, then splash]

[voice: dadi]
Bhasuraka leaped into the well with a great roar — and the deep, dark water swallowed him up. He never came out.

[pause]

The little rabbit hopped back to the jungle, and when the animals heard what had happened, they cheered and danced and celebrated. The jungle was free.

[sfx: birds singing, animals chattering, joyful sounds]

[music: warm, triumphant theme]

## Wisdom Moment

[music: gentle reflection]

[voice: dadi]
Beta, what do you think? The lion was so big, so strong, so fierce. And the rabbit was so small. But who won?

[pause]

You see, beta, strength is not everything. The lion had all the power in the jungle, but he had no wisdom. He was proud and angry, and his pride made him blind. The rabbit had no claws, no teeth, no roar — but he had a sharp mind and a brave heart.

[pause]

Remember that, beta. When you face a big problem — a problem that seems much bigger than you — don't just push against it. Think around it. Be clever. Be like the rabbit.

## Bilingual Coda

[music: soft, warm]

[voice: dadi]
Now, beta, Dadi wants to teach you a word. The word is "sher." Say it with me — sh-air.

[pause]

Sher.

[pause]

"Sher" means lion in Hindi. In India, we have real shers — the Asiatic lion. They live in a place called the Gir Forest, in Gujarat. They are magnificent, beautiful animals. Not cruel like Bhasuraka — real lions are just part of nature.

And you know what's interesting, beta? The word "sher" also means a couplet of poetry in Urdu. So a sher can be a lion, or it can be a poem. Isn't that something — the same word for the most powerful animal and the most beautiful words?

## Closing

[voice: dadi]
Thank you for sitting with me tonight, beta. I love telling you these old stories. Tomorrow, I'll tell you another one — this time about a golden swan and a lesson about greed.

[pause]

Good night, beta. Sleep well. And if you have a question for Dadi — anything at all — just ask me. I'm right here, under the banyan tree.

[music: gentle fade out]
[sfx: crickets, night ambience, fade to silence]
