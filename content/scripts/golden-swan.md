# The Golden Swan
## A Jataka Tale — Adapted for The Banyan Tree

[voice: dadi]
[music: gentle flute and veena, dawn ambience]

## Opening Framing

[sfx: morning birds, wind through reeds]

Come, beta, sit with Dadi. Do you know what color gold is? That warm, glowing color — like sunshine caught in metal? Tonight I want to tell you a story about something golden. Something magical. A swan whose feathers were made of pure gold.

[pause]

This story comes from the Jataka tales — very old stories from East India, stories about wisdom and kindness. Buddhist monks told these stories thousands of years ago, and they traveled across all of India, all the way to my village. My dadi told me this one on a rainy afternoon, while we watched the parrots shelter in the mango tree.

[pause]

This story is about a family, beta — a mother and her two daughters. And it is about a gift, and what happens when you want too much. Are you ready? Then let's begin.

[music: transition to storytelling theme]

## The Story

[sfx: gentle wind, distant temple bells]

[voice: dadi]
Long ago, in a small village near a wide, shining lake, there lived a poor family — a mother and her two daughters. They had a little hut with a thatched roof, and they worked very hard, but there was never quite enough to eat.

[pause]

Now beta, here is something you should know. Before these three were alone, there had been a father. He was a good man — kind and loving — and he had worried greatly about his family. When he fell ill and knew he would not get better, he made a wish with all his heart: "Let me come back. Let me find a way to take care of them, even after I am gone."

[sfx: soft, ethereal music]

[voice: dadi]
And beta — his wish was granted. But not in the way you might expect. He was reborn as a swan — a magnificent swan with feathers of pure, shimmering gold.

[pause]

The golden swan remembered his family. He remembered the little hut, the two daughters, the wife who worked so hard. And so one evening, as the sun was setting and the sky was pink and orange, the swan flew to their village.

[sfx: wings beating, gentle landing]

[voice: dadi]
The mother and daughters were sitting outside their hut, tired from the day's work. And suddenly — whoosh — a great swan landed before them. His feathers glowed like fire in the sunset.

[voice: swan]
"Do not be afraid. I was once your husband, your father. I have come back to help you. Here — take one of my feathers."

[voice: dadi]
The swan plucked one golden feather from his wing and laid it at the mother's feet. It gleamed in the fading light — real, solid gold.

[sfx: magical shimmer sound]

[voice: swan]
"Sell this feather. It will give you enough money for many weeks. And I will come back, again and again, and each time I will leave you one feather. You will never be poor again."

[music: warm, hopeful theme]

[voice: dadi]
And so it was, beta. Every few weeks, the golden swan would fly down from the sky, visit his family, and leave one shining feather. The mother would take it to the market and sell it, and they would have food, and clothes, and everything they needed.

[pause]

The daughters were so happy. They would run out to greet the swan, and he would nuzzle them with his golden head, and for a little while, it was almost like having their father back.

[sfx: children laughing, swan call]

[voice: dadi]
But beta — and here is where the story turns — the mother began to change.

At first she was grateful. Of course she was. One golden feather bought food for weeks. But slowly, slowly, a thought began to grow in her mind — like a weed growing between the bricks.

[pause]

[voice: mother]
"One feather at a time? Why should we wait? What if the swan stops coming? What if he forgets us? Next time he visits — I will take all his feathers at once. Then we will be rich forever."

[sfx: subtle tension music]

[voice: dadi]
Her daughters heard this and shook their heads.

"No, Mother," said the elder daughter. "The swan is kind to us. He comes because he loves us. We should not be greedy."

"He gives what he can give," said the younger daughter. "We should be thankful."

[voice: dadi]
But the mother would not listen, beta. Greed had planted its seed in her heart, and it had grown too big to pull out.

[pause]

[music: tension building]

The next time the golden swan landed in their yard, the mother rushed forward. Before the swan could even speak, she grabbed him — grabbed him tight — and began pulling out his feathers, one after another after another.

[sfx: frantic pulling, swan crying out]

[voice: swan]
"Stop! Please — stop! You cannot take them by force!"

[voice: dadi]
But it was too late. As the mother pulled each feather out, something terrible happened. The golden feathers — every single one — turned white. Plain, ordinary white. Not a speck of gold remained.

[sfx: shimmering sound fading to silence]

[voice: dadi]
The swan stood there, stripped and trembling, his beautiful golden plumage gone. He was just a white swan now — no magic, no gold, nothing.

[pause]

[voice: swan]
"I told you — one feather at a time, given freely, would stay gold. But taken by force, by greed, the gold disappears. I cannot help you anymore."

[voice: dadi]
And the swan spread his bare wings and flew away — slowly, heavily, over the lake, over the trees, into the clouds. And he did not come back.

[sfx: wings flapping, fading into distance]

[pause]

The mother stood there, holding a pile of plain white feathers, and she wept. Not because she was poor again — but because she understood what she had lost. Not gold, beta. She had lost trust. She had lost love. She had lost the gift that was freely given.

[music: bittersweet, gentle]

## Wisdom Moment

[music: gentle reflection]

[voice: dadi]
Beta, why do you think the feathers turned white?

[pause]

I'll tell you what I think. A gift that is given with love — freely, generously — that gift is golden. But the moment you grab, the moment you take more than what is offered, the moment greed takes over — the magic disappears.

[pause]

This is true with everything, beta. With gifts. With friendship. With love. You cannot force someone to give you more than they are ready to give. And if you try — you may lose everything.

## Bilingual Coda

[music: soft, warm]

[voice: dadi]
Now, beta, Dadi wants to teach you a word. The word is "sona." Say it with me — so-naa.

[pause]

Sona.

[pause]

"Sona" means gold in Hindi. But beta, here is something lovely — "sona" is also what we call someone we love very much. When Dadi calls you "mera sona" — my gold — it means you are precious to me, more precious than any golden feather.

In India, gold is very important. At weddings, brides wear gold jewelry — necklaces, bangles, earrings — all shining and beautiful. Gold means love, and prosperity, and good wishes. But the most precious sona, beta, is not the kind you wear. It's the kind that lives in your heart.

## Closing

[voice: dadi]
Thank you for sitting with me tonight, beta. That was a sad story in some ways, wasn't it? But it has an important lesson inside it, like a pearl inside an oyster.

[pause]

Tomorrow I'll tell you a funny story — about a clever fox and a talking cave. You'll like that one. It will make you laugh.

Good night, beta. Sleep well. And if you have a question for Dadi — anything at all — just ask me. I'm right here, under the banyan tree.

[music: gentle fade out]
[sfx: rain pattering softly, night ambience, fade to silence]
