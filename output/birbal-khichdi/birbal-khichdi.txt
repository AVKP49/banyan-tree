Beta, come sit with Dadi. Tonight I want to tell you about a very special king and his very clever minister. The king was Emperor Akbar — he ruled over a great empire in India, hundreds and hundreds of years ago. He had a beautiful palace with gardens and fountains, and people came from all over the world to see his court. But do you know what Akbar loved most? He loved riddles. He loved clever questions and clever answers. And the cleverest person in his whole court was a man named Birbal. Birbal was not a prince or a general. He was just a man with a sharp mind and a kind heart. And whenever there was a problem that nobody could solve, the king would say: "Send for Birbal." This story comes from the Akbar-Birbal tales — stories that families in North India have told for hundreds of years. My dadi told me this one while she was cooking khichdi — but I'll tell you what khichdi is at the end of the story. For now, let's begin.

One cold winter day, Emperor Akbar was sitting in his warm court, wrapped in a fine shawl. Outside, the wind howled and the Yamuna river was so cold that even the fish were shivering.

"Tell me," said the Emperor, looking around at his courtiers, "is there anyone in my kingdom so brave — or so foolish — that they would stand in the freezing Yamuna river all night long?"

The courtiers laughed. "No one, Your Majesty! It's impossible!" But then, beta, a poor man stepped forward from the back of the court. He was thin, his clothes were worn, and he had a family to feed. "I will do it, Your Majesty," he said quietly. "But if I do — will you give me a thousand gold coins?"

"A thousand gold coins! That's a lot of money. But yes — if you can stand in the river from sunset to sunrise, the gold is yours."

That night, the poor man walked to the river. The water was bitterly cold — so cold it burned his skin. He stepped in, deeper and deeper, until the water reached his neck. His teeth chattered. His body shook. The hours passed so slowly, beta — each minute felt like a year. But the poor man did not give up. Do you know why? Because far away, on a distant hill, he could see a tiny lamp burning in a palace window. Just a small, flickering light. And he stared at that light all through the long, cold night. It gave him something to look at, something to hold onto in his mind.

And when the sun rose — golden and warm over the river — the poor man was still standing there. He had done it. He had survived the whole night in the freezing water.

The poor man came to the court, wet and shivering but proud, to collect his thousand gold coins. But beta — here's where the story turns.

"Wait," said a courtier, stepping forward. "Your Majesty, this man cheated! He was staring at a lamp all night. The lamp's warmth helped him survive!"

Now, beta, that's silly, isn't it? A tiny lamp, far away on a hill — how could it warm anyone? But the courtier was jealous, and unfortunately, Emperor Akbar listened to him.

"If you were warmed by the lamp," said the Emperor, frowning, "then you did not truly withstand the cold. No gold coins for you."

The poor man was crushed. He had stood in freezing water all night, his body aching, his heart full of hope — and now they were taking away his reward because of a lamp miles away. He went to the one person he knew could help. He went to Birbal.

"Don't worry, my friend," said Birbal, with a small smile. "I have an idea."

The next day, something very unusual happened. Birbal did not come to court. The Emperor waited. The ministers waited. No Birbal.

"Where is Birbal? Send someone to fetch him!"

A messenger went to Birbal's house and came back with a puzzled look. "Your Majesty, Birbal says he cannot come to court today. He is busy cooking khichdi."

"Cooking khichdi? Birbal? Since when does Birbal cook? Tell him to come at once!"

But Birbal sent the same message again: "I am cooking khichdi. It is almost ready. Please come and see for yourself." Now, Akbar was curious — and when the Emperor is curious, the Emperor goes. So Akbar put on his fine coat and walked to Birbal's house.

And what did he see? Beta, he saw the funniest sight. In Birbal's courtyard, there was a very tall bamboo pole — as tall as a tree. At the very top of the pole hung a small pot. And far, far below the pot, on the ground, was a tiny fire — just a few small sticks burning with a tiny flame.

"Birbal! What is this? How do you expect to cook khichdi with the fire so far from the pot? The heat will never reach it!"

"Your Majesty," said Birbal, bowing with a gentle smile, "if a man can be warmed by a lamp burning on a distant hill across a river — then surely this fire can cook a pot of khichdi hanging a few feet above it."

And then, beta — the Emperor understood. His eyes went wide. And do you know what he did? He laughed. A big, real, honest laugh. Because Akbar was a good king — not a perfect king, but a good one. He knew when he was wrong, and he had the courage to say so.

"Birbal, you are right. I was unfair. The lamp could not have warmed that man, just as this fire cannot cook your khichdi. Send for the poor man — he will have his thousand gold coins today."

And so the poor man got his gold — every single coin. And Birbal? Birbal went back to court with a smile, having never raised his voice, never argued, never said "you are wrong, Your Majesty." He just showed the truth — gently, cleverly, with a pot of khichdi and a very tall pole.

Beta, have you ever had to be patient when something wasn't fair? Sometimes, when people are unfair, the bravest thing is not to shout or to be angry. Sometimes the bravest thing is to be clever — to find a way to show the truth so clearly that even the person who was wrong can see it and smile. That's what Birbal did. And that's why, hundreds of years later, we still tell his stories.

Now, beta, Dadi wants to teach you a word. The word is "khichdi." Say it with me — khich-di. Khich-di. Khichdi is a simple, comforting dish of rice and lentils, cooked together until they're soft and warm. When Dadi was small and feeling sick, her dadi would make her khichdi with a little ghee — that's clarified butter — and a squeeze of lemon. It's the food grandmothers make for grandchildren. It's simple, but it's made with love. Next time you're feeling a little cold or a little tired, ask your mom or dad to make you khichdi. And when you eat it, think of Birbal — and his very, very tall pole.
