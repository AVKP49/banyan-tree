Come, beta, come sit close to Dadi. Let me wrap this shawl around us both — there, cozy now? Tonight I have a very funny story for you. It's about a jackal — do you know what a jackal is? A jackal is like a small wild dog, with pointy ears and a bushy tail. In India, jackals live in the forests and they are very, very clever — sometimes too clever for their own good.  ... This jackal did something that no animal has ever done before or since. He pretended to be someone he was not — and for a little while, beta, he fooled everyone. Everyone! Even the tiger, even the elephant.  ... This story also comes from the Panchatantra — those same wise old stories I've been telling you. My dadi used to say: "This story is for anyone who has ever pretended to be something they're not." And then she would look at me with those knowing eyes of hers. Let me tell you what happened.

Once upon a time, in a thick jungle, there lived a jackal named Chandarava. Now, Chandarava was always hungry — always, beta. His ribs stuck out, his belly rumbled like thunder, and he could never find enough to eat in the jungle because the bigger animals chased him away from every meal.  ... One night, Chandarava was so desperately hungry that he did something very brave — or very foolish. He crept out of the jungle and into the nearby village to look for food.

But the village dogs heard him. They came running — big, fierce dogs with loud barks — and poor Chandarava ran for his life. He ran through narrow streets, past houses, around corners, with the dogs snapping at his heels.

And then — splash! — Chandarava fell right into a large vat of blue dye that a cloth-maker had left outside his house. The dye was deep and dark — the color of the midnight sky. Chandarava scrambled and splashed, but the dogs couldn't see him in the dark vat. They sniffed around and eventually wandered away.  ...

When Chandarava finally climbed out of the vat, he looked down at himself and — beta, he couldn't believe his own eyes. He was blue. Completely, brilliantly, beautifully blue. From the tip of his nose to the end of his tail — blue as a sapphire, blue as the summer sky.

Chandarava crept back into the jungle, and the first animals who saw him stopped dead in their tracks.

"What — what is that creature? I've never seen anything like it!"

"It's not a tiger, it's not a bear — look at that magnificent blue color! It must be a divine being!"

Beta, word spread through the jungle like fire. Every animal came to see the strange blue creature — the elephants, the tigers, the deer, the monkeys. They had never seen anything like him. And Chandarava — clever, hungry Chandarava — saw his chance.  ...

"Do not be afraid," said Chandarava in his deepest, most royal voice. "I have been sent by the gods themselves to be your raja — your king. I am a divine creature, and I have come to rule this jungle with wisdom and grace."

And do you know what, beta? They believed him. Every single one of them. The tiger bowed. The elephant knelt. Even the proud peacock spread his feathers in respect. They made Chandarava their raja — their king.

And oh, what a life Chandarava lived! The other animals brought him the finest food — fresh fruit, sweet honey, tender meats. He sat on a high rock like a throne, and the animals fanned him with large leaves. He gave orders and made pronouncements, and everyone obeyed.  ... But there was one thing Chandarava had to be very careful about. He had to stay away from the other jackals. Because if they came close, they might recognize him. They might see through the blue dye to the ordinary jackal underneath.

"Jackals are low creatures," Chandarava announced. "I do not wish to see them in my court. Send them away!"

And the other jackals were chased out of the kingdom. Imagine, beta — he sent away his own family, his own kind, just to keep his secret.  ...

Days passed. Weeks passed. The blue dye held, and Chandarava grew fatter and prouder with each day. He began to believe his own lie, beta. He began to think he really was a divine king.  ... But then one night — a clear, cool night with a full moon hanging over the jungle like a silver coin — something happened.

From beyond the hills, a pack of jackals began to howl. You know how jackals howl at the moon, beta? It's a long, rising call — awoooo — and when one jackal starts, every jackal for miles around feels the pull to join in. It's in their blood. It's who they are.  ... Chandarava was sitting on his rock throne, surrounded by tigers and elephants and deer. And he heard that howl. It went into his ears and down into his chest and straight into his heart.

He tried to resist. He clenched his jaw. He pressed his paws against the rock. But the howl was like a song he'd known since birth — he couldn't stop himself.  ...

"Awoooooo!"

Chandarava threw back his head and howled at the moon. A jackal's howl — unmistakable, plain, ordinary.

Every animal turned to stare. The tiger's eyes narrowed. The elephant lifted his trunk. The deer stamped their hooves. And the monkeys — oh, the monkeys were the first to shout:

"He's a jackal! He's just a common jackal! He tricked us all!"

Beta, the animals were furious. They chased Chandarava through the jungle — the same way the village dogs had chased him. He ran and ran and ran, the blue dye streaking behind him, until he disappeared into the darkest part of the forest, alone and hungry once more.  ...

And the jungle went back to the way it had always been — with no blue king on a rock, and a lesson that nobody ever forgot.

Beta, tell me — was Chandarava clever?  ... Yes, he was clever. Very clever. But clever is not the same as wise. Chandarava could fool everyone else — but he could not fool himself. Because in the end, a jackal is a jackal. And there is nothing wrong with being a jackal, beta. The mistake was not being a jackal. The mistake was pretending to be something he was not.  ... You are wonderful exactly as you are. Remember that.

Now, beta, Dadi wants to teach you a word. The word is "raja." Say it with me — ra-ja.  ... Ra-ja.  ... "Raja" means king in Hindi. In old India, there were many rajas — some were kind and wise, and some were not so wise. When Chandarava called himself a raja, he wanted the power and the respect. But a real raja, beta — a true king — earns that respect through honesty and goodness, not through tricks and blue dye. Next time you hear the word "raja," you'll know — it means king. And you'll remember that a true raja is honest about who they are.
