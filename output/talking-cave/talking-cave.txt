Once upon a time, in a dry, rocky part of the jungle, there lived a fox. He was not the biggest animal, and he was not the strongest. But oh, beta, he was clever. The cleverest animal in the whole jungle. His mind was always working — tick, tick, tick — like a little clock.  ... Every morning, the fox would leave his cave to find food. He would trot through the jungle, sniffing here, looking there, always careful, always alert. And every evening, he would come back to his cave — a nice, cool cave in the hillside, just the right size for a fox.

Now, in that same jungle, there lived a lion. And this lion was getting old. His legs were stiff. His eyes were not so sharp. He could not chase deer the way he used to. And he was getting very, very hungry.

One day, the lion had an idea — a lazy, sneaky idea.

"Why should I chase my food? I am the king! Let the food come to me. I'll find an empty cave, hide inside, and wait. When some animal comes home — I'll pounce!"

And so the lion searched the jungle until he found a cave. Can you guess whose cave it was, beta?  ... That's right — the fox's cave. The lion squeezed inside, crouched in the shadows, and waited. He waited all day long, quiet as a stone, while the fox was out looking for food.

When evening came, the fox trotted home, humming a little song to himself. But as he got close to his cave, he stopped.  ... Something was wrong.

The fox looked at the ground in front of his cave. He saw paw prints — big paw prints — going into the cave. Big, heavy, lion-sized paw prints. And then he looked more carefully. The paw prints went in — but no paw prints came out.  ... Beta, do you understand? Something big had gone into his cave, and it was still in there.

Now, a foolish animal might have walked right in. But our fox? Our clever, clever fox? He sat down at a safe distance and he thought. Tick, tick, tick. And then — he smiled.

"Hello, cave!"

The fox called out in a loud, cheerful voice.

"Hello, cave! How are you today?"

Silence. Nothing. The fox called again.

"Cave! Why aren't you greeting me? Every single evening when I come home, you call out to me. You say, 'Welcome home, fox! Come in, come in!' Have you forgotten? Why are you so quiet today?"  ...

Now, beta — the cave had never talked. Not once. Not ever. Caves don't talk! But the fox said it so naturally, so casually, as if it were the most normal thing in the world. And inside the cave, the lion was listening.

The lion thought to himself: "Hmm. The cave must usually greet the fox. If the cave stays silent, the fox will know something is wrong. He won't come in, and I'll go hungry. I better answer for the cave!"  ... And so, beta — the lion did the silliest thing. He opened his big mouth and tried to sound like a cave.

"Welcome home, fox! Come in, come in!"

Oh, beta! Can you imagine? A lion, trying to sound like a cave! His deep, rumbling voice echoed and boomed through the jungle. Every bird flew away. Every squirrel scrambled up a tree. It was the loudest, most ridiculous "welcome home" anyone had ever heard.  ... And the fox? The fox burst out laughing.

"Ha! Thank you, 'cave,' but I think I'll sleep outside tonight! I've never heard of a cave with a roar before!"

And with that, the fox turned around and ran — fast, fast, fast — away from the cave, away from the lion, laughing all the way. The lion crawled out of the cave, hungry and embarrassed, and slunk away into the jungle. He never tried that trick again.

Beta, why was the fox so clever?  ... Because he paid attention. He noticed the paw prints. He noticed that tracks went in but didn't come out. He didn't panic. He didn't run in without thinking. He stopped, he observed, and he made a plan.  ... And here's the really clever part — he set a trap with words. He said something that wasn't true — "the cave always talks to me" — and the lion believed it. The fox used the lion's own hunger and foolishness against him. So remember, beta: always pay attention to what's around you. Look at the signs. Think before you act. And if something doesn't feel right — trust that feeling.

Now, beta, Dadi wants to teach you a word. The word is "gufa." Say it with me — goo-faa.  ... Gufa.  ... "Gufa" means cave in Hindi. India has some of the most beautiful gufas in the whole world. There are the Ajanta caves, where ancient artists painted the most magnificent pictures on the walls — pictures of princes and princesses and animals, thousands of years old. And the Elephanta caves, on an island near Mumbai, with giant stone sculptures carved right into the rock. Imagine, beta — people made art inside gufas, long, long ago, and we can still see it today. That's the magic of a gufa — it protects things and keeps them safe for a very long time.
