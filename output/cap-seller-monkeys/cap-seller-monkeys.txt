Beta! Come sit, come sit — Dadi has the funniest story for you tonight. Are you ready to laugh? Because this story makes me laugh every single time, even though I've heard it a hundred times. My dadi told it to me, her dadi told it to her, and I think grandmothers in India have been telling this story and laughing at it for hundreds and hundreds of years.  ... This is a story about a man who sold topis — caps, hats. And about a troupe of monkeys who were very, very naughty. You know monkeys, beta — they copy everything. If you scratch your head, they scratch their head. If you wave your hand, they wave their hand. They are the best copycats in the whole world.  ... And that, beta, is exactly what gets them — and the cap seller — into trouble. Are you ready? Let's begin.

Long, long ago, in a small village in India, there lived a cap seller. He was a simple man — kind, hardworking, with a big bushy mustache and a warm smile. Every day, he would stack his caps on top of his head — red caps, blue caps, yellow caps, green caps — one on top of the other, in a big tall tower, and walk from village to village selling them.  ...

"Topis! Topis for sale! Fine caps for the sun, fine caps for the rain! Topis!"

He would walk for miles, beta, with that tall stack of colorful caps on his head, perfectly balanced. It's harder than it sounds — try stacking ten caps on your head and walking! But our cap seller had been doing it for years. He was an expert.  ...

One very hot afternoon, the cap seller was walking along a dusty road between two villages. The sun was blazing, and he was very tired. He came to a big, shady banyan tree — oh beta, what a tree it was! Its branches spread out like a green umbrella, and its shade was cool and inviting.

"I'll just rest here for a few minutes," the cap seller said to himself. "Just a short nap. Then I'll go on."

He sat down with his back against the trunk of the banyan tree, with all his caps still stacked on his head. And in just a moment — because that shade was so pleasant and the breeze was so gentle — he fell fast asleep.  ...

Now, beta, what the cap seller did not know was that this banyan tree was home to a big troupe of monkeys. They had been sitting up in the branches, watching him with their bright, curious eyes.

The monkeys looked at the cap seller. They looked at his caps — all those beautiful, colorful caps stacked in a tower on his head. And monkeys, as I told you, love to copy. They love to take things and play with them. They are the naughtiest creatures in the jungle.  ... One by one, quietly, slowly, the monkeys climbed down. They reached out with their clever little fingers and — one by one — they took the caps right off the sleeping cap seller's head.

Each monkey took a cap and put it right on their own head. A monkey in a red cap. A monkey in a blue cap. A monkey in a yellow cap. Up they climbed, back into the tree, wearing the caps proudly, chattering and laughing at each other.  ...

The cap seller slept and slept. When he finally woke up — yawning, stretching — he reached up to touch his caps and... nothing. His head was bare except for his own worn-out topi.

"My caps! Where are my caps?"

He looked left. He looked right. He looked behind the tree. No caps anywhere. And then — he looked up.

Beta, imagine what he saw! Fifteen monkeys sitting in the branches of the banyan tree, each one wearing one of his caps, grinning down at him.

"You rascals! Give me back my caps! Those are not yours!"

But the monkeys just chattered and made faces at him. The cap seller shook his fist at them. The monkeys shook their fists right back. He stamped his foot. The monkeys stamped their feet. He waved his arms. The monkeys waved their arms.  ...

"Shoo! Go away! Give me my caps!"

But the more he shouted and waved, the more the monkeys copied him — shouting and waving right back. They thought it was the most wonderful game.

The cap seller was getting desperate, beta. Those caps were everything he had. Without them, he couldn't sell anything. Without selling, he couldn't eat. He tried jumping up and down. The monkeys jumped up and down. He tried making scary faces. The monkeys made scary faces — and honestly, beta, the monkeys were better at it.  ... He sat down under the tree, his head in his hands. What could he do? He wasn't strong enough to climb the tree. He couldn't catch fifteen monkeys. He was just a simple cap seller with a big mustache and no caps.  ...

And then, beta — the cap seller had an idea. A beautiful, simple idea. You see, he remembered what everyone knows about monkeys: they copy everything. Every single thing.  ... The cap seller stood up slowly. He looked at the monkeys. The monkeys looked at him. Then, very deliberately, very slowly, he reached up to his head — took off his own topi — and threw it on the ground.  ...

And the monkeys — every single monkey — reached up, grabbed their cap, and threw it on the ground!

Red caps, blue caps, yellow caps, green caps — raining down from the tree like colorful rain! The cap seller laughed — oh, how he laughed! — and quickly gathered up every single cap, stacked them back on his head, and walked off down the road before those naughty monkeys could change their minds.

"Topis! Topis for sale!"

And the monkeys sat in their tree, scratching their heads, wondering where all their lovely new hats had gone.

Beta, what do you think? How did the cap seller win?  ... He didn't win by being bigger or stronger. He didn't win by shouting or fighting. He won by understanding. He watched the monkeys, he understood how they behaved, and he used that understanding to solve his problem. That's what smart people do, beta — they watch, they think, and they find a way.  ... And sometimes the cleverest solution is the simplest one.

Now, beta, Dadi wants to teach you a word. The word is "topi." Say it with me — to-pi.  ... To-pi.  ... "Topi" means a cap or a hat in Hindi. In India, there are so many different kinds of topis! There's the Gandhi topi — a simple white cap named after Mahatma Gandhi. There's the Nehru topi. In Rajasthan, men wear bright, colorful turbans called pagris. And in my village, the old men wore simple white topis when they went to the temple. Next time you put on a hat or a cap, you can call it your topi. And maybe — just maybe — keep it away from any monkeys you see.
